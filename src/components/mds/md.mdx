## 1. Core Design Principles
The design of RaUF is motivated by key physical characteristics of radar sensing:
- Anisotropic Uncertainty: Due to the limited number of radar antennas, azimuth uncertainty is substantially larger than range uncertainty, yielding a characteristic crescent-shaped spatial distribution of measurements.
- Doppler Consistency: Reflections from true targets satisfy kinematic constraints, whereas artifacts induced by clutter or multipath effects typically deviate from these constraints.

## 2. Key Technical Modules
### Bayesian Probabilistic Model (BPM)
To address the ill-posed geometric inference caused by sparse measurements, RaUF reformulates the task as jointly estimating a localization predictor $f_{\theta}(x)$ and an uncertainty quantification model $g_{\varphi}(x)$.
- Anisotropic Covariance: In polar coordinates, the model predicts radial uncertainty $\sigma_{r}$ and angular uncertainties $\sigma_{\alpha}, \sigma_{\beta}$.
- Loss Function: A negative log-likelihood (NLL) objective is adopted to learn fine-grained confidence distributions in target regions, rather than enforcing a rigid deterministic mapping.

### Bidirectional Domain Attention Fusion (BDAF)
This module exploits the complementarity between spatial structure and Doppler information to strengthen feature representations:
- Stage 1 (Spatial to Doppler): Spatial cues are used as queries to enhance Doppler features, emphasizing regions consistent with radar ego-motion.
- Stage 2 (Doppler to Spatial): The enhanced Doppler information is projected back to the spatial domain to guide spatial feature reconstruction and effectively suppress artifacts.

### Frustum-based Voxelization Strategy
To provide effective supervision for uncertainty learning, RaUF expands frustum regions along radar rays in range, azimuth, and elevation dimensions. All LiDAR points falling within each frustum are treated as potential reflections, enabling the construction of occupancy ground truth that captures intrinsic radar measurement uncertainty.
